## Introduction

Long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs) constitute the largest class of transcribed elements in the human genome and have emerged in the past decade as a distinct layer of post-transcriptional and transcriptional regulation, with roles documented in development, immune cell differentiation, and disease (Mattick et al., 2023; Statello et al., 2021). Compared with protein-coding mRNAs, lncRNAs are characteristically lower in steady-state abundance, often short-lived (Tani et al., 2012), and more cell-type-restricted in their expression (Cabili et al., 2011). These properties have practical consequences for translational lncRNA research: a regulator whose biological function is concentrated in a small effector subset may be functionally important in vivo while being effectively undetectable in standard bulk-tissue transcriptomics, where its per-cell contribution is averaged across thousands of bystander cells. This implies that standard bulk-tissue RNA-seq catalogs may systematically under-report lncRNAs whose activity is confined to rare immune effector subsets.

We recently reported that *IDI2-AS1*, an antisense lncRNA transcribed from the *IDI2* locus, modulates expression of the type-2 effector cytokine *IL5* in human cells (Endo et al., 2025). Using siRNA-mediated knock-down in HuT78 T-cells and orthogonal validation in A549 epithelial cells, we showed that loss of *IDI2-AS1* increases steady-state *IL5* mRNA, identifying the lncRNA as a candidate negative regulator of *IL5* expression. Although several other lncRNAs have been implicated in type-2 / allergic inflammation, *IDI2-AS1* is mechanistically distinct in that it directly targets *IL5*, the most clinically tractable type-2 cytokine.

The *IL5* axis is one of the rare success stories of molecularly targeted therapy in allergic disease, with multiple anti-IL5 / anti-IL5Rα biologics now in clinical use (Lambrecht et al., 2019; detailed clinical context is expanded in the Discussion). The clinical efficacy of these protein-level interventions establishes that *IL5* is dose-limiting for the eosinophilic phenotype in vivo, which in turn motivates a search for upstream regulators — including non-coding regulators — that might extend the therapeutic landscape, particularly in directions where the cell-type-specificity of an oligonucleotide-based modulator could complement the systemic effects of an antibody.

A natural next step after our in vitro report was therefore to ask whether the *IDI2-AS1* / *IL5* axis is detectable, and operates in the predicted direction, in patient tissue from human IL5-driven allergic diseases. Public bulk RNA-seq cohorts now span the four major type-2 inflammatory diseases — asthma (Sajuthi et al., 2020), atopic dermatitis (Tsoi et al., 2019), eosinophilic esophagitis (Sherrill et al., 2014; Kleuskens et al., 2024), and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis (Peng et al., 2019) — at sample sizes that, in the largest cohort, exceed several hundred patients. Here, we re-analyzed five public bulk RNA-seq datasets under a uniform pipeline (Fig. 1) to test three in vivo questions about the in vitro mechanism: (i) is *IDI2-AS1* differentially expressed between patients and controls in any of these tissues; (ii) does sample-level *IDI2-AS1* abundance correlate with *IL5* abundance in the direction predicted by the in vitro repression model (negative); and (iii) if the bulk-tissue signal departs from the in vitro prediction, can any of it be attributed to eosinophil- and Th2-associated shifts in tissue cell composition? We hypothesized that any per-cell repressive *IDI2-AS1* → *IL5* effect operating in allergic effector cells would be largely masked in bulk tissue by disease-associated changes in eosinophil and Th2 cell abundance, and designed the analysis to separate these two contributions. In addressing these questions, the present study also offers a reusable cell-composition-adjusted re-analysis framework applicable to other low-abundance, cell-type-restricted lncRNA candidates evaluated against bulk-tissue cohorts.
