## 1. Introduction

Long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs) constitute the largest class of transcribed elements in the human genome and have emerged in the past decade as a distinct layer of post-transcriptional and transcriptional regulation, with roles documented in development, immune cell differentiation, and disease (Mattick et al., 2023; Statello et al., 2021). Compared with protein-coding mRNAs, lncRNAs are characteristically lower in steady-state abundance, more cell-type-restricted in their expression, and more sensitive to extraction and quantification choices (Cabili et al., 2011; Mukherjee et al., 2017). These properties have practical consequences for translational lncRNA research: a regulator whose biological function is concentrated in a small effector subset may be functionally important in vivo while being effectively undetectable in standard bulk-tissue transcriptomics, where its per-cell contribution is averaged across thousands of bystander cells.

We recently reported that *IDI2-AS1*, an antisense lncRNA transcribed from the *IDI2* locus, modulates expression of the type-2 effector cytokine *IL5* in human cells (Endo et al., 2025). Using siRNA-mediated knock-down in HuT78 T-cells and orthogonal validation in A549, we showed that loss of *IDI2-AS1* increases steady-state *IL5* mRNA, identifying the lncRNA as a candidate negative regulator of *IL5* expression in lymphoid lineages. *IDI2-AS1* joins a small set of lncRNAs implicated in type-2 / allergic inflammation (e.g. *MIR222HG* in airway smooth muscle proliferation, Liu et al., 2018; *GATA3-AS1* in Th2 differentiation, Gibbons et al., 2018), but is mechanistically distinct in that it directly targets the most clinically tractable type-2 cytokine.

The *IL5* axis is, in fact, one of the rare success stories in molecularly targeted therapy of allergic disease. Three monoclonal biologics — mepolizumab and reslizumab (anti-IL5) and benralizumab (anti-IL5Rα) — are now approved for severe eosinophilic asthma and increasingly for related type-2 indications including eosinophilic esophagitis and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis (Bel et al., 2014; Bachert et al., 2019; Hirano et al., 2019; Lambrecht et al., 2019). The clinical efficacy of this protein-level intervention establishes that *IL5* is dose-limiting for the eosinophilic phenotype in vivo, which in turn motivates a search for upstream regulators — including non-coding regulators — that might extend the therapeutic landscape, particularly in directions where the cell-type-specificity of an oligonucleotide-based modulator could complement the systemic effects of an antibody.

A natural next step after our in vitro report was therefore to ask whether the *IDI2-AS1* / *IL5* axis is detectable, and operates in the predicted direction, in patient tissue from human IL5-driven allergic diseases. Public bulk RNA-seq cohorts now span the four major type-2 inflammatory diseases — asthma (Sajuthi et al., 2020), atopic dermatitis (Tsoi et al., 2018), eosinophilic esophagitis (Sherrill et al., 2014; Kleuskens et al., 2024), and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis (Wang et al., 2019) — at sample sizes that, in the largest cohort, exceed several hundred patients. Re-analyzing these cohorts under a uniform pipeline allows three distinct in vivo questions to be asked of the in vitro mechanism: (i) is *IDI2-AS1* itself differentially expressed between patients and controls in any of these tissues; (ii) does sample-level *IDI2-AS1* abundance correlate with *IL5* abundance in the direction predicted by the in vitro repression model (negative); and (iii) if the bulk-tissue signal departs from the in vitro prediction, can the deviation be attributed in a formally testable way to differences in the cellular composition of the biopsies? In this study, we address all three questions across five datasets (*n* = 856 samples), and report that the bulk readout differs from the in vitro prediction in a specific and biologically interpretable way: *IDI2-AS1* is invariant at the tissue scale, the *IDI2-AS1*↔*IL5* sample-level association runs opposite to the cell-line direction, and ≈ 90 % of that association is attributable to shared expression in the eosinophil compartment, leaving a within-tissue residual effect indistinguishable from zero. We argue that this is the result that should be expected when a cell-type-restricted lncRNA regulator is interrogated by bulk RNA-seq, and we use the *IDI2-AS1* / *IL5* contrast to make a more general case for single-cell follow-up of in vitro-discovered lncRNA mechanisms.
