## Methods

### Ethics

This study used only publicly available, fully de-identified RNA-seq datasets that were obtained with appropriate ethical approval by the original investigators. No new human or animal data were generated, and no additional ethical approval was required for the present re-analysis.

### Dataset selection and acquisition

Public bulk-tissue RNA-seq datasets from human IL5-driven allergic diseases were identified by querying the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) and ArrayExpress in March 2026. Inclusion criteria were: (i) human disease tissue with paired or matched controls; (ii) RNA-seq (not microarray) at sufficient depth to detect long noncoding RNAs; (iii) availability of per-sample expression matrices (raw counts or FPKM) — datasets providing only pre-computed differential-expression tables were excluded; and (iv) detection of *IDI2-AS1* (ENSG00000260196) as a non-zero feature in the supplementary expression matrix. After applying these criteria, five datasets covering four allergic diseases were retained for analysis (Table 1): asthma (GSE152004, *n* = 695 nasal epithelial brushings; Sajuthi et al., 2020); atopic dermatitis (GSE121212, *n* = 65 skin biopsies; Tsoi et al., 2019); eosinophilic esophagitis (GSE246323, *n* = 10 esophageal biopsies, Kleuskens et al., 2024; and GSE58640, *n* = 16 esophageal biopsies, Sherrill et al., 2014); and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis (GSE136825, *n* = 70 nasal polyp and turbinate biopsies; Peng et al., 2019). Three additional candidate datasets (GSE201955, GSE65832, GSE179269) were excluded because *IDI2-AS1* was absent from the available processed matrix or because per-sample raw data could not be recovered without re-quantification from SRA. For GSE136825, only the patient nasal-polyp biopsies (NP, *n* = 42) and unrelated healthy-donor inferior-turbinate biopsies (Control IT, *n* = 28) were used; the within-patient paired turbinate samples (NP_IT, *n* = 33) were excluded so that case/control contrasts in this cohort were structurally comparable to those in the other four datasets. Sample-to-condition mapping was reconstructed for every dataset from the GEO series matrix metadata; assignments are documented in `data/metadata/final_datasets.csv` and the per-dataset loader scripts (`code/R/loaders/load_GSE*.R`). We acknowledge that inclusion criterion (iv) restricts the conclusions of this study to cohorts in which *IDI2-AS1* is detectably expressed at the bulk level, and that datasets in which the transcript is absent from the processed matrix (whether for biological or technical reasons) are by construction not represented.

### Differential expression analysis

For the four raw-count datasets (GSE152004, GSE121212, GSE246323, GSE136825), low-count features (rowSum < 10 across the dataset, a conventional DESeq2 pre-filter used to remove genes with extremely sparse counts that are unlikely to yield stable fold-change estimates) were removed and differential expression was computed with DESeq2 (v1.42.1; Love et al., 2014) under a single-factor design (`~ group`, control as reference) using the Wald test with Benjamini–Hochberg FDR correction. Effect sizes for the patient-vs-control contrast were stabilized by adaptive shrinkage with apeglm (Zhu et al., 2019; `lfcShrink(coef = "group_patient_vs_control", type = "apeglm")`), and the resulting log<sub>2</sub> fold-changes and posterior standard errors were used as the input statistics for downstream meta-analysis. For the FPKM dataset (GSE58640), expression was log<sub>2</sub>(FPKM + 1)-transformed and analyzed with limma (v3.58.1; Ritchie et al., 2015) using the `limma-trend` workflow (`lmFit` followed by `eBayes(trend = TRUE)`); coefficient standard errors were recovered as `stdev.unscaled[, 2] · sigma`. A focal-gene panel covering the lncRNA of interest (*IDI2-AS1*, ENSG00000260196), four comparator lncRNAs (*MIR22HG*, *GABPB1-AS1*, *OIP5-AS1*, *LITATS1* — selected because the same lncRNAs were previously identified by our group as bacterial- and viral-stress-responsive in human cells; Abe et al., 2023; Yagi et al., 2024), and six type-2 / inflammatory cytokines (*IL5*, *IL4*, *IL13*, *IL6*, *TNF*, *IFNG*) was extracted from each per-dataset table for figure construction; the full per-dataset DEG tables are provided in `results/tables/`. Of the four comparator lncRNAs, *LITATS1* fell below the rowSum count threshold in all symbol-keyed allergic-tissue datasets and is therefore reported only at the level of dataset-wise availability and not in the cross-disease comparator analysis.

### Cross-disease meta-analysis

Per-dataset patient-vs-control log<sub>2</sub> fold-changes and standard errors for each focal gene were combined across the available cohorts using a random-effects meta-analysis (REML estimator, `metafor::rma`, v4.6-0; Viechtbauer, 2010). Heterogeneity was quantified with Cochran's *Q* and *I*<sup>2</sup>. Forest plots for *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* were generated with `metafor::forest` (Fig. 2). The Ensembl-ID gene rows for *IDI2-AS1* (ENSG00000260196) and *IL5* (ENSG00000113525) — which appear in the two Ensembl-keyed datasets (GSE246323 and GSE136825) — were collapsed onto their HGNC symbols prior to pooling so that each gene contributes at most one effect estimate per dataset. Genes matched only by HGNC symbol (the comparator lncRNAs *MIR22HG*, *GABPB1-AS1*, *OIP5-AS1* and the cytokines *IL13*, *IL6*, *TNF*, *IFNG*, *IL4*) were pooled across the three symbol-keyed datasets in which they passed the count-filter (typically k = 3, with k = 2 for *IL4*, which was below the count threshold in atopic-dermatitis skin). *IDI2-AS1* was pooled across all five datasets (k = 5) by virtue of being matched on both its HGNC symbol and its Ensembl ID; *IL5* was pooled across four (k = 4; below threshold in GSE246323).

### Sample-level correlation and cell-type-adjusted analysis

Within each dataset, per-sample *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* abundance was extracted from variance-stabilized counts (`DESeq2::vst(blind = TRUE)`, raw-count datasets) or from log<sub>2</sub>(FPKM + 1) values (GSE58640). Pairwise Spearman correlation between *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* was computed per dataset (Fig. 3; Table 3). To test whether any bulk-tissue *IDI2-AS1*↔*IL5* covariation could be attributed to shared expression in allergic-effector cells, two compartment-specific marker-gene signatures were constructed from canonical eosinophil markers (CLC, EPX, PRG2, RNASE2, RNASE3, SIGLEC8, CCR3) and Th2 / type-2 markers (GATA3, IL4R, CCR4, PTGDR2, IL13, IL17RB). For each sample, a signature score was computed as the mean of the per-gene *z*-scores across the panel (per-dataset standardization). Cell-type-adjusted Spearman partial correlation between *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* was then estimated with `ppcor::pcor.test` (Kim, 2015), conditioning on both signature scores (Fig. 4 and Supplementary Fig. 1; Table 3). For the largest cohort (GSE152004 asthma, *n* = 695), a mediation-style decomposition was performed with `mediation::mediate` (v4.5.0; Tingley et al., 2014) using *IDI2-AS1* as treatment, the eosinophil signature score as mediator, and *IL5* as outcome (1,000 nonparametric bootstrap resamples; percentile confidence intervals). The eosinophil signature was used as the sole mediator in this decomposition — rather than including the Th2 signature in parallel — because eosinophils are the cellular endpoint of the IL5 axis, are quantitatively the dominant *IL5*-expressing population among circulating and tissue-infiltrating leukocytes in active allergic disease, and were the compartment that most strongly attenuated the partial correlation in the asthma cohort. Because the underlying data are cross-sectional observational measurements, the resulting ACME and ADE estimates should be interpreted as a statistical decomposition under the assumed mediator–outcome model, not as identification of a causal effect in the strict counterfactual sense. Mediator and outcome models were fit by ordinary least squares (`m_model: eos_score ~ idi2as1`; `o_model: il5 ~ idi2as1 + eos_score`), and the bootstrap-based average mediated effect (ACME, in the sense of `mediation::mediate`), average direct effect (ADE), and proportion mediated were extracted from the bootstrap distribution. The marker-signature scoring approach was chosen instead of CIBERSORTx (Newman et al., 2019) for full local reproducibility from the public count matrices; CIBERSORTx-ready CPM/FPKM input matrices are nonetheless provided in `results/intermediate/cibersortx_input/` for independent verification with the LM22 reference (`code/R/08_prepare_cibersortx_input.R`).

### Specificity heatmaps

To place the *IDI2-AS1* result in the context of related lncRNAs and the broader type-2 cytokine network, two heatmaps were produced with ComplexHeatmap (v2.18.0; Gu et al., 2016) and displayed as panels A (lncRNAs) and B (cytokines) of Fig. 5. For each panel, per-dataset patient-vs-control log<sub>2</sub> fold-changes were assembled into a gene × dataset matrix; rows were ordered with the focal gene (*IDI2-AS1* or *IL5*) on top, columns were ordered by disease, and disease and total-*n* annotations were drawn above the heatmap. The color scale was symmetric around zero and clipped at ±4 log<sub>2</sub> units to keep the *IL13*-in-AD outlier (log<sub>2</sub>FC ≈ 4.9) from compressing the visible dynamic range of the rest of the panel.

### Code and data availability

All analysis code, intermediate result tables, and figure-generating scripts are publicly available at https://github.com/hidenori-tani/IDI2-AS1-allergy (commit hash recorded at submission) under an MIT license. Software environment is captured by `renv.lock` (R 4.3.2, Bioconductor 3.18). All input data are public GEO datasets cited above; no new sequencing data were generated for this study.
