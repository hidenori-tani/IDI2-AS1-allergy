## Introduction

Long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs) constitute the largest class of transcribed elements in the human genome and have emerged in the past decade as a distinct layer of post-transcriptional and transcriptional regulation, with roles documented in development, immune cell differentiation, and disease (Mattick et al., 2023; Statello et al., 2021). Compared with protein-coding mRNAs, lncRNAs are characteristically lower in steady-state abundance, often short-lived (Tani et al., 2012), and more cell-type-restricted in their expression (Cabili et al., 2011). These three properties have direct practical consequences for translational lncRNA research from bulk transcriptomic data: a regulator whose biological function is concentrated in a small effector subset may be functionally important in vivo while being effectively undetectable in standard bulk-tissue RNA-seq, where its per-cell contribution is averaged across thousands of bystander cells. Standard bulk-tissue catalogs therefore systematically under-report lncRNAs whose activity is confined to rare immune effector subsets, and a null result in such a catalog cannot be interpreted as evidence against in vivo regulatory function.

The interpretive problem is sharper than simple under-detection. Bulk-tissue RNA-seq measures a population-weighted average across every cell type in the biopsy, so two distinct quantities collapse onto the same readout for any cell-type-restricted regulator: (i) the *per-cell* expression of the regulator inside its effector subset, and (ii) the *abundance* of that subset within the biopsy. In a diseased tissue these two quantities can move in opposite directions. For a putative repressor of a type-2 effector cytokine, per-cell levels of the regulator would be expected to fall in active disease (consistent with a knock-down → target ↑ in vitro phenotype), while the effector compartment that co-expresses both the regulator and its target *expands*. A naive sample-level correlation between regulator and target across bulk samples will then track compositional expansion rather than per-cell regulation, and may flip in sign relative to the per-cell effect. The standard differential-expression and bulk-correlation workflow, applied without explicit cell-composition adjustment, cannot distinguish "the regulator is not active in this disease" from "the regulator is active per-cell but masked by composition." There is currently no standardized framework that disentangles these two contributions using bulk transcriptomes alone.

Here we present a reusable, cell-composition-adjusted re-analysis pipeline designed to address this interpretive gap (Fig. 1). The pipeline combines (i) uniform DESeq2/limma differential-expression analysis across heterogeneous cohorts and pooling by random-effects meta-analysis; (ii) marker-signature-based deconvolution of disease-relevant effector-cell abundance, with CIBERSORTx-formatted inputs released so that readers can confirm the conclusion with the LM22 reference panel; (iii) partial-correlation analysis of sample-level regulator–target covariation, conditioning on the cell-composition signature; and (iv) a bootstrap mediation decomposition that splits the bulk-level regulator–target association into a mediated (cell-composition) component and a direct (within-tissue, per-cell-surrogate) component. The framework is intentionally restricted to information that can be recovered from bulk RNA-seq alone, so that it can be applied retrospectively to the large body of clinically annotated bulk transcriptomic data already in the public domain.

We demonstrate the framework on *IDI2-AS1*, a recently identified in vitro repressor of *IL5* expression (Endo et al., 2025), as a worked example. The choice is methodologically deliberate. First, *IDI2-AS1* is exactly the class of candidate for which the interpretive problem above is acute: it is a low-abundance, cell-type-restricted lncRNA whose published mechanism (loss-of-function → *IL5* ↑ in HuT78 T-cells and A549 epithelial cells) predicts a per-cell direction that is opposite to the direction in which the *IDI2-AS1*-expressing effector compartment is known to shift in active type-2 disease. Second, *IL5* is one of the few cytokine axes in allergic disease for which protein-level interventions are clinically validated — multiple anti-IL5 / anti-IL5Rα biologics are now in routine clinical use (Lambrecht et al., 2019) — so identification of authentic upstream regulators of *IL5*, including non-coding regulators, has translational interest independent of the methodological argument. Third, public bulk RNA-seq cohorts span all four major IL5-driven type-2 inflammatory diseases — asthma (Sajuthi et al., 2020), atopic dermatitis (Tsoi et al., 2019), eosinophilic esophagitis (Sherrill et al., 2014; Kleuskens et al., 2024), and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis (Peng et al., 2019) — at sample sizes that, in the largest cohort, exceed several hundred patients, supplying the statistical scale needed to demonstrate each step of the pipeline.

We applied the pipeline to five such cohorts (combined *n* = 856) to ask three questions that together exercise its components: (i) is bulk *IDI2-AS1* differentially expressed between patients and controls in any of these tissues; (ii) does sample-level *IDI2-AS1* abundance covary with *IL5* in the direction predicted by the in vitro repression model; and (iii) when the bulk-tissue signal departs from the in vitro prediction, can the cell-composition-adjustment step recover an interpretation consistent with the in vitro mechanism, by attributing the bulk association to compositional rather than direct effects? Our purpose is not to relitigate the in vitro *IDI2-AS1* → *IL5* mechanism, which we treat as an independent finding (Endo et al., 2025), but to use this candidate as a transparent test case in which the pipeline must reconcile a bulk-tissue null with an in vitro per-cell effect of known direction. The framework that emerges is directly applicable to other low-abundance, cell-type-restricted lncRNA candidates evaluated against bulk-tissue cohorts.
