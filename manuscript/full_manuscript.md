## Abstract

**Background.** The long noncoding RNA *IDI2-AS1* was recently identified as a negative regulator of interleukin 5 (*IL5*) in human T-cell-line knock-down experiments (Endo et al., 2025). Because *IL5* is the validated target of three approved anti-eosinophil biologics, an in vivo demonstration of the *IDI2-AS1* / *IL5* axis in patient tissue from IL5-driven allergic disease has direct translational interest.

**Methods.** Five public bulk RNA-seq cohorts (856 samples total) covering asthma (GSE152004, *n* = 695), atopic dermatitis (GSE121212, *n* = 65), eosinophilic esophagitis (GSE246323, *n* = 10; GSE58640, *n* = 16), and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis (GSE136825, *n* = 70) were re-analyzed under a uniform pipeline (DESeq2 + apeglm; limma-trend for FPKM) and combined by random-effects meta-analysis. Sample-level *IDI2-AS1*↔*IL5* Spearman correlations were re-estimated as partial correlations conditioning on per-sample eosinophil and Th2 marker-signature scores. In the asthma cohort, bootstrap causal mediation (1,000 simulations) decomposed the association into direct (ADE) and mediated (ACME) effects.

**Results.** Tissue-bulk *IDI2-AS1* expression was essentially invariant in all five cohorts; the random-effects pooled log<sub>2</sub>FC was +5.1 × 10<sup>−5</sup> (95 % CI [−0.005, +0.005]; *I*<sup>2</sup> = 0.26 %; *Q* *p* = 0.47), a converging null. Sample-level *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* covaried *positively* in every testable cohort, significantly so in asthma (Spearman ρ = +0.109, *p* = 0.004) — opposite to the in vitro repressive direction. Conditioning on the eosinophil signature attenuated the asthma association to a partial ρ of +0.052 (*p* = 0.17). Causal mediation in the asthma cohort attributed ≈ 90 % of the covariation to the eosinophil compartment (ACME = +0.109, 95 % CI [+0.041, +0.176], *p* = 0.002), with a within-tissue direct effect indistinguishable from zero (ADE = +0.012, *p* = 0.76). Comparator lncRNAs and canonical type-2 cytokines recovered the expected disease-stratified pattern in the same datasets.

**Conclusions.** The in vitro *IDI2-AS1* → *IL5* axis is not contradicted by patient transcriptomes — it is masked by them: cell-composition mixing dilutes the per-cell signal and generates a spurious positive covariation via shared eosinophil expression. Single-cell follow-up is the next step. The analysis positions *IDI2-AS1* as a candidate for cell-type-targeted RNA therapeutics in the *IL5* axis.

**Keywords.** lncRNA; IDI2-AS1; IL5; allergic inflammation; asthma; atopic dermatitis; eosinophilic esophagitis; nasal polyposis; bulk RNA-seq; mediation analysis; cell composition; type-2 immunity.
## 1. Introduction

Long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs) constitute the largest class of transcribed elements in the human genome and have emerged in the past decade as a distinct layer of post-transcriptional and transcriptional regulation, with roles documented in development, immune cell differentiation, and disease (Mattick et al., 2023; Statello et al., 2021). Compared with protein-coding mRNAs, lncRNAs are characteristically lower in steady-state abundance and more cell-type-restricted in their expression (Cabili et al., 2011). These properties have practical consequences for translational lncRNA research: a regulator whose biological function is concentrated in a small effector subset may be functionally important in vivo while being effectively undetectable in standard bulk-tissue transcriptomics, where its per-cell contribution is averaged across thousands of bystander cells.

We recently reported that *IDI2-AS1*, an antisense lncRNA transcribed from the *IDI2* locus, modulates expression of the type-2 effector cytokine *IL5* in human cells (Endo et al., 2025). Using siRNA-mediated knock-down in HuT78 T-cells and orthogonal validation in A549, we showed that loss of *IDI2-AS1* increases steady-state *IL5* mRNA, identifying the lncRNA as a candidate negative regulator of *IL5* expression. Although several other lncRNAs have been implicated in type-2 / allergic inflammation, *IDI2-AS1* is mechanistically distinct in that it directly targets *IL5*, the most clinically tractable type-2 cytokine.

The *IL5* axis is, in fact, one of the rare success stories in molecularly targeted therapy of allergic disease. Three monoclonal biologics — mepolizumab and reslizumab (anti-IL5) and benralizumab (anti-IL5Rα) — are now approved for severe eosinophilic asthma and increasingly for related type-2 indications including eosinophilic esophagitis and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis (Bel et al., 2014; Bachert et al., 2017; Hirano et al., 2019; Lambrecht et al., 2019). The clinical efficacy of this protein-level intervention establishes that *IL5* is dose-limiting for the eosinophilic phenotype in vivo, which in turn motivates a search for upstream regulators — including non-coding regulators — that might extend the therapeutic landscape, particularly in directions where the cell-type-specificity of an oligonucleotide-based modulator could complement the systemic effects of an antibody.

A natural next step after our in vitro report was therefore to ask whether the *IDI2-AS1* / *IL5* axis is detectable, and operates in the predicted direction, in patient tissue from human IL5-driven allergic diseases. Public bulk RNA-seq cohorts now span the four major type-2 inflammatory diseases — asthma (Sajuthi et al., 2020), atopic dermatitis (Tsoi et al., 2019), eosinophilic esophagitis (Sherrill et al., 2014; Kleuskens et al., 2024), and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis (Peng et al., 2019) — at sample sizes that, in the largest cohort, exceed several hundred patients. Re-analyzing these cohorts under a uniform pipeline allows three distinct in vivo questions to be asked of the in vitro mechanism: (i) is *IDI2-AS1* itself differentially expressed between patients and controls in any of these tissues; (ii) does sample-level *IDI2-AS1* abundance correlate with *IL5* abundance in the direction predicted by the in vitro repression model (negative); and (iii) if the bulk-tissue signal departs from the in vitro prediction, can the deviation be attributed in a formally testable way to differences in the cellular composition of the biopsies? In this study, we address all three questions across five datasets (*n* = 856 samples), and report that the bulk readout differs from the in vitro prediction in a specific and biologically interpretable way: *IDI2-AS1* is invariant at the tissue scale, the *IDI2-AS1*↔*IL5* sample-level association runs opposite to the cell-line direction, and ≈ 90 % of that association is attributable to shared expression in the eosinophil compartment, leaving a within-tissue residual effect indistinguishable from zero. We argue that this is the result that should be expected when a cell-type-restricted lncRNA regulator is interrogated by bulk RNA-seq, and we use the *IDI2-AS1* / *IL5* contrast to make a more general case for single-cell follow-up of in vitro-discovered lncRNA mechanisms.
## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1 Dataset selection and acquisition

Public bulk-tissue RNA-seq datasets from human IL5-driven allergic diseases were identified by querying the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) and ArrayExpress in March 2026. Inclusion criteria were: (i) human disease tissue with paired or matched controls; (ii) RNA-seq (not microarray) at sufficient depth to detect long noncoding RNAs; (iii) availability of per-sample expression matrices (raw counts or FPKM) — datasets providing only pre-computed differential-expression tables were excluded; and (iv) detection of *IDI2-AS1* (ENSG00000260196) as a non-zero feature in the supplementary expression matrix. After applying these criteria, five datasets covering four allergic diseases were retained for analysis (Table 1): asthma (GSE152004, *n* = 695 nasal epithelial brushings; Sajuthi et al., 2020); atopic dermatitis (GSE121212, *n* = 65 skin biopsies; Tsoi et al., 2019); eosinophilic esophagitis (GSE246323, *n* = 10 esophageal biopsies, Kleuskens et al., 2024; and GSE58640, *n* = 16 esophageal biopsies, Sherrill et al., 2014); and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis (GSE136825, *n* = 70 nasal polyp and turbinate biopsies; Peng et al., 2019). Three additional candidate datasets (GSE201955, GSE65832, GSE179269) were excluded because *IDI2-AS1* was absent from the available processed matrix or because per-sample raw data could not be recovered without re-quantification from SRA. For GSE136825, only the patient nasal-polyp biopsies (NP, *n* = 42) and unrelated healthy-donor inferior-turbinate biopsies (Control IT, *n* = 28) were used; the within-patient paired turbinate samples (NP_IT, *n* = 33) were excluded so that case/control contrasts in this cohort were structurally comparable to those in the other four datasets. Sample-to-condition mapping was reconstructed for every dataset from the GEO series matrix metadata; assignments are documented in `data/metadata/final_datasets.csv` and the per-dataset loader scripts (`code/R/loaders/load_GSE*.R`).

### 2.2 Differential expression analysis

For the four raw-count datasets (GSE152004, GSE121212, GSE246323, GSE136825), low-count features (rowSum < 10) were removed and differential expression was computed with DESeq2 (v1.42.1) under a single-factor design (`~ group`, control as reference) using the Wald test with Benjamini–Hochberg FDR correction. Effect sizes for the patient-vs-control contrast were stabilized by adaptive shrinkage with apeglm (`lfcShrink(coef = "group_patient_vs_control", type = "apeglm")`), and the resulting log<sub>2</sub> fold-changes and posterior standard errors were used as the input statistics for downstream meta-analysis. For the FPKM dataset (GSE58640), expression was log<sub>2</sub>(FPKM + 1)-transformed and analyzed with limma (v3.58.1) using the `limma-trend` workflow (`lmFit` followed by `eBayes(trend = TRUE)`); coefficient standard errors were recovered as `stdev.unscaled[, 2] · sigma`. A focal-gene panel covering the lncRNA of interest (*IDI2-AS1*, ENSG00000260196), four comparator lncRNAs (*MIR22HG*, *GABPB1-AS1*, *OIP5-AS1*, *LITATS1*) and six type-2 / inflammatory cytokines (*IL5*, *IL4*, *IL13*, *IL6*, *TNF*, *IFNG*) was extracted from each per-dataset table for figure construction; the full per-dataset DEG tables are provided in `results/tables/`.

### 2.3 Cross-disease meta-analysis

Per-dataset patient-vs-control log<sub>2</sub> fold-changes and standard errors for each focal gene were combined across the available cohorts using a random-effects meta-analysis (REML estimator, `metafor::rma`, v4.6-0). Heterogeneity was quantified with Cochran's *Q* and *I*<sup>2</sup>. Forest plots for *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* were generated with `metafor::forest` (Figure 2). The Ensembl-ID gene rows for *IDI2-AS1* (ENSG00000260196) and *IL5* (ENSG00000113525) — which appear in the two Ensembl-keyed datasets (GSE246323 and GSE136825) — were collapsed onto their HGNC symbols prior to pooling so that each gene contributes at most one effect estimate per dataset. Genes matched only by HGNC symbol (the comparator lncRNAs *MIR22HG*, *GABPB1-AS1*, *OIP5-AS1* and the cytokines *IL13*, *IL6*, *TNF*, *IFNG*, *IL4*) were pooled across the three symbol-keyed datasets in which they passed the count-filter (typically k = 3, with k = 2 for *IL4*, which was below the count threshold in atopic-dermatitis skin). *IDI2-AS1* was pooled across all five datasets (k = 5) by virtue of being matched on both its HGNC symbol and its Ensembl ID; *IL5* was pooled across four (k = 4; below threshold in GSE246323).

### 2.4 Sample-level correlation and cell-type-adjusted analysis

Within each dataset, per-sample *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* abundance was extracted from variance-stabilized counts (`DESeq2::vst(blind = TRUE)`, raw-count datasets) or from log<sub>2</sub>(FPKM + 1) values (GSE58640). Pairwise Spearman correlation between *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* was computed per dataset (Table 2). To test whether any tissue-bulk *IDI2-AS1*↔*IL5* covariation could be attributed to shared expression in allergic-effector cells, two compartment-specific marker-gene signatures were constructed from canonical eosinophil markers (CLC, EPX, PRG2, RNASE2, RNASE3, SIGLEC8, CCR3) and Th2 / type-2 markers (GATA3, IL4R, CCR4, PTGDR2, IL13, IL17RB). For each sample, a signature score was computed as the mean of the per-gene *z*-scores across the panel (per-dataset standardization). Cell-type-adjusted Spearman partial correlation between *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* was then estimated with `ppcor::pcor.test`, conditioning on both signature scores. For the largest cohort (GSE152004 asthma, *n* = 695), a formal causal-mediation analysis was performed with `mediation::mediate` (v4.5.0) using *IDI2-AS1* as treatment, the eosinophil signature score as mediator, and *IL5* as outcome (1,000 nonparametric bootstrap resamples; percentile confidence intervals). Mediator and outcome models were fit by ordinary least squares (`m_model: eos_score ~ idi2as1`; `o_model: il5 ~ idi2as1 + eos_score`), and the average causal mediation effect (ACME), average direct effect (ADE), and proportion mediated were extracted from the bootstrap distribution. The marker-signature scoring approach was chosen instead of CIBERSORTx for full local reproducibility from the public count matrices; CIBERSORTx-ready CPM/FPKM input matrices are nonetheless provided in `results/intermediate/cibersortx_input/` for independent verification with the LM22 reference (`code/R/08_prepare_cibersortx_input.R`).

### 2.5 Specificity heatmaps

To place the *IDI2-AS1* result in the context of related lncRNAs and the broader type-2 cytokine network, two heatmaps were produced with ComplexHeatmap (v2.18.0). For each panel, per-dataset patient-vs-control log<sub>2</sub> fold-changes were assembled into a gene × dataset matrix; rows were ordered with the focal gene (*IDI2-AS1* or *IL5*) on top, columns were ordered by disease, and disease and total-*n* annotations were drawn above the heatmap. The color scale was symmetric around zero and clipped at ±4 log<sub>2</sub> units to keep the *IL13*-in-AD outlier (log<sub>2</sub>FC ≈ 4.9) from compressing the visible dynamic range of the rest of the panel.

### 2.6 Code and data availability

All analysis code, intermediate result tables, and figure-generating scripts are publicly available at https://github.com/[username]/IDI2-AS1-allergy (commit hash recorded at submission) under an MIT license. Software environment is captured by `renv.lock` (R 4.3.2, Bioconductor 3.18). All input data are public GEO datasets cited above; no new sequencing data were generated for this study.
## 3. Results

### 3.1 Tissue-bulk *IDI2-AS1* expression is invariant across four IL5-driven allergic diseases

To test whether the in vitro *IDI2-AS1* → *IL5* repressive axis described in our recent T-cell-line study (Endo et al., 2025) is reflected in patient tissues, we re-analyzed five public bulk RNA-seq cohorts spanning four allergic diseases (Table 1; total *n* = 856 samples). Per-dataset patient-vs-control differential expression for *IDI2-AS1* yielded effect sizes that were small in every cohort and statistically significant in none: log<sub>2</sub>FC ranged from −0.234 (atopic dermatitis, GSE121212; *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 0.41) to +0.247 (eosinophilic esophagitis, GSE58640; *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 0.15), with intermediate values in the asthma (+0.0015), CRSwNP (−2.8 × 10<sup>−6</sup>), and EoE-Kleuskens (−0.036) cohorts. A random-effects meta-analysis pooling all five datasets returned an overall log<sub>2</sub>FC of +5.1 × 10<sup>−5</sup> (95 % CI [−0.005, +0.005], *p* = 0.98) with negligible heterogeneity (*I*<sup>2</sup> = 0.26 %, Cochran's *Q* *p* = 0.47; Figure 2A). Importantly, this null is not a failure of the pipeline: in the same datasets we recovered the canonical type-2 / inflammatory signature with the expected direction and magnitude — *IL13* was strongly upregulated in atopic dermatitis (log<sub>2</sub>FC = +4.92, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 2 × 10<sup>−30</sup>) and in EoE (Sherrill, log<sub>2</sub>FC = +0.91, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 2 × 10<sup>−5</sup>); *IL6* and *TNF* were significantly elevated in AD (log<sub>2</sub>FC = +3.25 and +0.89; both *p*<sub>adj</sub> < 10<sup>−7</sup>); and *IL5* itself was upregulated in asthma (log<sub>2</sub>FC = +0.42, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 0.014) and EoE-Sherrill (log<sub>2</sub>FC = +0.61, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 0.002). The five-dataset *I*<sup>2</sup> ≈ 0 % observation for *IDI2-AS1* therefore reflects a robust convergent finding — *all* cohorts independently report no tissue-bulk effect — rather than noise or pipeline artifact.

### 3.2 *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* covary positively at the tissue-bulk level — opposite to the cell-line prediction

We next asked whether the within-cell repressive relationship between *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* documented in Endo et al. (2025) is detectable as a sample-level correlation in the four cohorts where both transcripts are reliably quantified (Figure 3, Table 2). Contrary to the cell-line prediction of a negative association, all four datasets returned a *positive* Spearman correlation between *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5*: ρ = +0.109 (*p* = 0.004) in the asthma cohort (GSE152004, *n* = 695 — by far the largest and best-powered); ρ = +0.489 (*p* = 0.054) in EoE (GSE58640, *n* = 16); ρ = +0.085 (*p* = 0.48) in CRSwNP; and ρ = +0.060 (*p* = 0.63) in atopic dermatitis. The directional inversion between the published in vitro mechanism (knock-down → IL5 ↑, predicting negative in-tissue correlation) and the in vivo bulk readout (positive correlation, significant in the largest cohort) is the central observation that motivates the cell-composition analysis of the next section.

### 3.3 The tissue-level *IDI2-AS1*↔*IL5* association is mediated by eosinophil composition

A natural explanation for the inverted-direction tissue signal is that bulk RNA-seq mixes effector cells in which both *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* are co-expressed (Th2 lymphocytes, ILC2s, eosinophils) with bystander tissue cells in which neither is expressed; samples with greater allergic infiltration would then carry up *both* transcripts in proportion, producing a spurious positive correlation that is driven by composition rather than within-cell regulation. To test this directly, we built per-sample eosinophil and Th2 marker-signature scores (mean *z*-score across 7 eosinophil markers and 6 Th2 markers; Methods §2.4) and re-estimated the *IDI2-AS1*↔*IL5* relationship as a partial Spearman correlation conditioning on both signatures (Table 2, Figure 4). Adjustment substantially attenuated the raw association in every cohort and abolished it in the asthma dataset where it had reached significance: the asthma Spearman ρ dropped from +0.109 (*p* = 0.004) to a partial ρ of +0.052 (*p* = 0.17), the EoE ρ dropped from +0.489 to +0.425 (*p* = 0.13), the CRSwNP ρ moved slightly from +0.085 to +0.135 (*p* = 0.27), and the AD ρ collapsed from +0.060 to +0.010 (*p* = 0.94). To formalize the contribution of the eosinophil compartment specifically, we performed a bootstrap causal-mediation analysis in the asthma cohort (1 000 simulations; *IDI2-AS1* as treatment, eosinophil signature as mediator, *IL5* as outcome). The average causal mediation effect through eosinophil composition was ACME = +0.109 (95 % CI [+0.041, +0.176], *p* = 0.002), while the average direct effect — the within-tissue residual *IDI2-AS1* → *IL5* association after holding the eosinophil signature constant — was ADE = +0.012 (95 % CI [−0.075, +0.106], *p* = 0.76); the proportion mediated was 0.90. In other words, ≈ 90 % of the apparent positive *IDI2-AS1*↔*IL5* covariation in the largest available patient cohort is statistically attributable to shared eosinophil abundance, and the residual within-tissue direct effect is indistinguishable from zero. Mediation in the smaller cohorts was less informative because of limited power: the atopic-dermatitis cohort returned a directionally concordant but non-significant ACME (+0.054, *p* = 0.10); the CRSwNP cohort returned a non-significant ACME of opposite sign (−0.028, *p* = 0.13); and the smaller EoE cohort (GSE58640, *n* = 16) was skipped because it fell below the prespecified 20-sample mediation threshold. The mediation conclusion therefore rests on the asthma cohort, where the sample size affords adequate power; the smaller cohorts cannot confirm or refute it on their own.

### 3.4 Specificity: comparator lncRNAs respond to disease whereas *IDI2-AS1* does not

To test whether the flat tissue-bulk profile of *IDI2-AS1* is a genuine biological feature or a generic property of all expressed lncRNAs in these tissues, we examined three comparator lncRNAs — *MIR22HG*, *GABPB1-AS1*, and *OIP5-AS1* — in the three datasets in which they were quantified by HGNC symbol (GSE152004 asthma, GSE121212 atopic dermatitis, GSE58640 eosinophilic esophagitis); the two Ensembl-keyed datasets (GSE246323, GSE136825) do not carry these lncRNAs under symbol matching and were not included for the comparators (Figure 5). Each of the three comparators showed clear, disease-specific differential expression. *MIR22HG* was strongly upregulated in atopic-dermatitis skin (log<sub>2</sub>FC = +0.82, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 1 × 10<sup>−16</sup>) and in EoE (log<sub>2</sub>FC = +0.60, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 1 × 10<sup>−4</sup>) but moved little in asthmatic nasal epithelium (log<sub>2</sub>FC = −0.02). *GABPB1-AS1* was downregulated in two of three cohorts, reaching strong significance in atopic dermatitis (log<sub>2</sub>FC = −0.50, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 5 × 10<sup>−3</sup>) and trending negative in asthma (log<sub>2</sub>FC = −0.05, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 0.06). *OIP5-AS1* was modestly but significantly downregulated in atopic dermatitis (log<sub>2</sub>FC = −0.19, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 8 × 10<sup>−3</sup>). Pooled meta-analysis (k = 3 datasets per gene) confirmed nontrivial cross-disease signal for the comparators: *MIR22HG* pooled log<sub>2</sub>FC = +0.46 (*I*<sup>2</sup> = 97 %), *GABPB1-AS1* pooled log<sub>2</sub>FC = −0.10 (*I*<sup>2</sup> = 97 %), *OIP5-AS1* pooled log<sub>2</sub>FC = −0.03 (*I*<sup>2</sup> = 85 %). Against this background, *IDI2-AS1*, which uniquely was quantified in all five datasets (k = 5) by virtue of also being matched on its Ensembl ID, returned the lowest pooled effect size (+5.1 × 10<sup>−5</sup>) and, uniquely, near-zero between-study heterogeneity (*I*<sup>2</sup> = 0.26 %). The flatness of *IDI2-AS1* at the tissue-bulk level is therefore a property of this lncRNA specifically, not a generic feature of low-abundance lncRNAs in allergic tissue.

### 3.5 Specificity: the type-2 cytokine network is appropriately disease-stratified

A complementary specificity check on the cytokine side confirmed that the analysis captures known disease biology in the expected pattern (Figure 6). *IL13* was the strongest disease-discriminating cytokine in atopic dermatitis (log<sub>2</sub>FC = +4.92, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 2 × 10<sup>−30</sup>) and was also significantly elevated in EoE (Sherrill log<sub>2</sub>FC = +0.91, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 2 × 10<sup>−5</sup>), as expected for the canonical type-2 effector. *IL5* was significantly elevated in asthma (+0.42, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 0.014) and in EoE-Sherrill (+0.61, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 0.002), consistent with eosinophil recruitment. *IL6* and *TNF* were strongly elevated in AD only (log<sub>2</sub>FC = +3.25 and +0.89; *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 3 × 10<sup>−7</sup> and 6 × 10<sup>−8</sup>), in line with that disease's broader inflammatory profile. *IFNG* was significantly downregulated in asthmatic nasal epithelium (log<sub>2</sub>FC = −0.66, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 5 × 10<sup>−7</sup>) but upregulated in atopic-dermatitis skin (log<sub>2</sub>FC = +1.43, *p*<sub>adj</sub> = 6 × 10<sup>−3</sup>), reflecting the well-known difference in IFN-γ involvement between airway and cutaneous type-2 inflammation. The cytokine panel therefore behaves as expected from established immunology, providing a positive control against which the specific flatness of *IDI2-AS1* in the same cohorts can be interpreted with confidence.
## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Summary of findings

We tested whether the *IDI2-AS1* → *IL5* repressive axis identified in a human T-cell-line knock-down system (Endo et al., 2025) is detectable in patient tissues across four IL5-driven allergic diseases — asthma, atopic dermatitis, eosinophilic esophagitis, and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis — using five public bulk RNA-seq cohorts spanning 856 samples. Three results stand out. First, tissue-bulk *IDI2-AS1* expression is essentially invariant between patients and controls in every cohort, and the random-effects pooled fold-change is centered on zero with negligible between-study heterogeneity (*I*<sup>2</sup> = 0.26 %). Second, sample-level *IDI2-AS1* and *IL5* abundance instead covary *positively*, significantly so in the largest cohort (asthma, *n* = 695), which is the opposite of the within-cell repressive direction predicted by the in vitro mechanism. Third, formal causal mediation in that same cohort attributes ≈ 90 % of the apparent positive covariation to shared expression in the eosinophil compartment, leaving a within-tissue direct effect that is statistically indistinguishable from zero (ADE *p* = 0.76). Together, these results refine — rather than confirm — the simple expectation that the in vitro axis would be visible as differential lncRNA expression at the tissue scale, and they identify cell-composition mixing as the specific reason the published mechanism is not recoverable from bulk transcriptomes.

### 4.2 Biological significance: why the in vitro mechanism is invisible to bulk RNA-seq

Bulk tissue RNA-seq measures a population-weighted average of every cell type present in the biopsy. For a regulator whose function is restricted to a small effector subset — here, presumably Th2 lymphocytes, type-2 innate lymphoid cells, mast cells, and eosinophils — two distinct quantities collapse onto the same readout: (i) the per-cell expression level inside the relevant subset, and (ii) the abundance of that subset within the biopsy. In disease tissues, both can change, and they do so in opposite directions for a putative repressor of a type-2 effector cytokine: per-cell *IDI2-AS1* would be expected to fall in active disease (consistent with the cell-line knock-down → IL5 ↑ result), while the eosinophil/Th2 compartment expands. The first effect is diluted by the second, and a simple bulk mean, computed across thousands of bystander epithelial or stromal cells in which neither *IDI2-AS1* nor *IL5* is meaningfully expressed, is biased toward zero. Our cell-type-adjusted analysis dissects these two contributions explicitly: when the eosinophil signature is held constant, the residual *IDI2-AS1*↔*IL5* relationship in the largest cohort vanishes (ADE = +0.012, *p* = 0.76), which is exactly what would be expected if the entire bulk signal were composition-driven and the within-effector regulatory direction is masked by — not contradicted by — the bulk readout. This interpretation is also consistent with the absence of *IDI2-AS1* from previously published differential-expression catalogs of these diseases: it is unlikely that a regulator of *IL5* has gone unnoticed for a decade by accident, and much more likely that the granularity at which most clinical RNA-seq studies operate is simply the wrong one for this specific molecule.

### 4.3 Implications for in vivo discovery of cell-type-specific lncRNA regulators

The case made here for *IDI2-AS1* generalizes beyond a single transcript. lncRNAs are characteristically lower-abundance and more cell-type-restricted than protein-coding genes (Mattick et al., 2023), which means the per-cell signal of an authentic regulator is more easily diluted in bulk tissue than the signal of a typical mRNA. A reasonable corollary is that searching for "lncRNAs that are differentially expressed in disease tissue" — the standard catalog operation in clinical transcriptomics — will systematically miss regulators that act inside small effector subsets, even when their downstream protein-coding targets (here, *IL5*, *IL13*) are themselves easily detected as differentially expressed. The *IDI2-AS1* / *IL5* contrast is therefore a useful illustration of why future discovery of in vivo lncRNA function in immune disease should preferentially use single-cell or spatial assays, and why a null result for an interesting lncRNA in a bulk catalog should be interpreted as "not seen at this granularity" rather than "not biologically active." Existing single-cell airway atlases (Travaglini et al., 2020) provide the natural follow-up substrate: testing whether *IDI2-AS1* is enriched in eosinophils, ILC2s, or Th2 cells in patient airway tissue, and whether its per-cell expression in those subsets falls in active disease, would be the directly informative experiment.

### 4.4 Clinical and translational context

*IL5* is the validated target of three anti-eosinophil biologics (mepolizumab and reslizumab, anti-IL5; benralizumab, anti-IL5Rα), all approved for severe eosinophilic asthma and, increasingly, for related type-2 indications including EoE and CRSwNP (Bel et al., 2014; Bachert et al., 2017; Hirano et al., 2019). The clinical success of this axis is direct evidence that *IL5* is dose-limiting for the eosinophilic phenotype, which in turn implies that any *upstream* regulator of *IL5* — including a non-coding regulator — is mechanistically of interest. Our results do not, by themselves, support *IDI2-AS1* as an immediate clinical biomarker: tissue-bulk expression is flat, so circulating or biopsy-derived bulk *IDI2-AS1* is unlikely to stratify patients. They do, however, position the lncRNA as a candidate in the rapidly maturing field of cell-type-targeted RNA therapeutics: an oligonucleotide-based modulator delivered to Th2/eosinophil lineages would have a defined molecular target whose downstream relevance has already been validated by the existing biologics. In this framing the present manuscript serves as the in vivo-context companion to the earlier in vitro mechanistic report and as a brief against over-interpreting bulk-tissue null results when the candidate molecule is cell-type-restricted.

### 4.5 Limitations and future directions

Several caveats accompany this analysis. First, we relied entirely on public datasets that were not designed for lncRNA discovery; library preparation choices (poly-A selection, ribo-depletion), sequencing depth, and read length differ between cohorts and may shape the per-dataset detection of low-abundance transcripts even when an effect would otherwise be present. Second, the cell-type-adjustment uses a marker-gene signature score rather than a deconvolution algorithm such as CIBERSORTx. We chose this approach for full local reproducibility from the same count matrices, and we provide CIBERSORTx-formatted inputs (`results/intermediate/cibersortx_input/`) so that readers can confirm the conclusion with the LM22 reference panel; nonetheless, the marker-score approach treats the eosinophil and Th2 compartments as one-dimensional summaries and cannot resolve subtle redistribution within those compartments. Third, the formal mediation analysis is well-powered only in the asthma cohort (*n* = 695); the directionally consistent but underpowered estimates in atopic dermatitis, CRSwNP, and EoE call for replication in larger samples. Fourth, the central biological interpretation — that per-cell *IDI2-AS1* falls in active disease but is masked by simultaneous expansion of the *IDI2-AS1*-expressing compartment — is a hypothesis derived from the bulk data, not a direct measurement; the decisive experiment is single-cell or spatial transcriptomics of patient tissue from the same diseases. Finally, the present work does not retest the in vitro *IDI2-AS1* → *IL5* mechanism itself, which was established for the HuT78 / A549 systems in our prior report (Endo et al., 2025) and would benefit from independent replication in additional cell systems; the present study asks only whether the published mechanism manifests at the tissue scale, and concludes that it does not — for reasons that are themselves biologically informative. Future work should (i) re-examine *IDI2-AS1* and its regulatory circuit in single-cell airway and skin atlases, (ii) test whether *IDI2-AS1* knock-down in primary human Th2 cells phenocopies the cell-line *IL5* phenotype, and (iii) explore whether oligonucleotide-based modulation of *IDI2-AS1* in cell-type-targeted delivery systems can recapitulate the IL5-axis attenuation that anti-IL5 biologics achieve through the protein-level pathway.
## 5. Conclusion

Across five public RNA-seq cohorts (*n* = 856) covering asthma, atopic dermatitis, eosinophilic esophagitis, and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis, the lncRNA *IDI2-AS1* — recently identified as an in vitro repressor of *IL5* — shows no patient-vs-control differential expression at the tissue-bulk level (pooled log<sub>2</sub>FC ≈ 0, *I*<sup>2</sup> = 0.26 %), yet its sample-level abundance covaries positively with *IL5*. In the largest cohort (asthma, *n* = 695), formal causal mediation attributes ≈ 90 % of that covariation to shared expression in the eosinophil compartment, leaving no detectable within-tissue direct effect (ADE *p* = 0.76). The in vitro *IDI2-AS1* → *IL5* axis is therefore not contradicted by bulk patient transcriptomes — it is masked by them. Recovering the regulatory direction in vivo will require single-cell or spatial assays of the same diseases, which we propose as the next mechanistic step.
